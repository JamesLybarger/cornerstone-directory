# SECTION 1: MARKETING STRATEGY

---

## Phase 1 — Pre-Launch (Weeks 1–2)

**Goal:** Build anticipation and a warm audience before the directory opens. Establish James as the recognizable voice behind it. Let people feel the "why" before they ever see a price.

### Core Pre-Launch Mindset

Before asking anyone to join, give them a reason to care. The pre-launch window is not about the product — it is about the problem. Surface the pain of Christian business owners who have no good place to find and support each other, or who are paying $400+ per year for directories that don't deliver community.

### Facebook (Primary Pre-Launch Channel)

- **Post the founder story** in James's own voice — why he felt called to build this, what gap he saw, what he hopes it becomes. Long-form, personal, no sales pitch. Pin it to the top of his profile.
- **Create a Facebook Group** — "Cornerstone Directory — Founding Members" — and invite his personal network. This becomes the community holding pen before launch.
- **Daily warm-up posts** for 14 days: share thoughts on Christian business, ask questions ("What's the biggest challenge you face as a Christian entrepreneur?"), share scripture that applies to business life.
- **Soft poll:** "If there was a trusted directory of Christian-owned businesses in your area — would you use it to find a plumber? Attorney? Designer?" Plant the concept before the ask.

### Instagram

- Begin posting 5–7 days before launch with quote graphics from Psalm 118:22 and related scriptures. No pitch — pure value and identity.
- Short Reels or Stories with James speaking directly to camera: "I want to ask you a question — have you ever specifically tried to find a Christian-owned business to hire?" Start the conversation.
- Add a link in bio to a waitlist landing page or the directory itself with a "coming soon" note.

### TikTok

- Post 2–3 raw, unpolished "here's what I'm building" videos. Authenticity matters more than production value on TikTok. No B-roll needed — just James talking into his phone.
- Lead with the problem, not the product: "Christian business owners are paying $400 a year for directories that do basically nothing. I'm about to change that."

### LinkedIn

- Publish a 500–800 word article titled: "There are 40 million Christians in the American workforce. So why is it so hard to find each other in business?"
- Position the piece as thought leadership — no product pitch in the article. End with: "I'm building something to solve this. Follow along."
- Connect with Christian business groups, Faith Driven Entrepreneur alumni, and Christian Chamber of Commerce members.

### Email / Direct Outreach

- James personally messages 50–100 people in his existing network — fellow church members, consulting clients, Christian business contacts. Not a mass email — individual messages that say: "I'm about to launch something and I thought of you specifically."
- Keep the message short: one sentence of what it is, one sentence of the founding member opportunity, a link.

### Pre-Launch Metrics to Watch

- Facebook Group members added
- Instagram/TikTok follower growth
- Waitlist signups (if applicable)
- Direct message responses

## Phase 2 — Founding 500 Push (Weeks 3–6)

**Goal:** Fill the 500 founding member spots at the $59.99 lifetime rate. Create real urgency without manufactured hype.

### The Founding 500 Frame

The Founding 500 is the cornerstone of the cornerstone. These are the people who believed before it was obvious. Lean into the honor of that — this is not "a sale," it is an invitation to help build something meaningful. Communicate that the lifetime deal disappears forever when spot 500 is claimed. After that, it's $59.99/year.

### Facebook

- **Daily countdown posts** once spots are below 200: "137 founding spots remaining." Not manipulative — just honest. People need to know the window is closing.
- **Testimonial posts** as early members join: ask the first 20 members if James can share their business and why they joined. Screenshot their words (with permission). Real people are more persuasive than any copy.
- **Facebook Live** in Week 4 or 5 — James goes live in the Founding Members Group, answers questions, walks through the directory, talks about the vision. Keep it under 30 minutes, stay conversational.
- **Referral activation:** Announce in the group that every member who refers a friend gets a $4.99 credit toward future renewal. Give them language to share — a short paragraph they can copy and paste.

### Instagram

- Reels in Week 3: "Why I priced this at $59.99 for life" — address the price transparency head-on. Show the competitor prices. Let the contrast speak for itself.
- Stories: Founding member countdowns (use the poll sticker: "Are you in? — Yes / Still thinking"), behind-the-scenes of building the site.
- User-generated content ask: "If you just joined as a founding member, post your business and tag us — we'll reshare every one."

### TikTok

- Post the competitor comparison video (script in Section 2) — this is the clearest value proposition and tends to perform well.
- Response videos: if people comment with questions, James records a reply directly to that comment. TikTok rewards this kind of engagement.
- Stitch or duet relevant Christian business content from other creators.

### LinkedIn

- Post the founding member opportunity framing (see script in Section 2) — LinkedIn professionals respond to ROI framing. "One year of membership paid for itself if you close one deal through the directory."
- Tag relevant Christian business groups and professional networks in the post.
- DM outreach to Christian business owners James is connected with — personal, not templated.

### Church & Network Outreach (see dedicated section below)

Phase 2 is also when to activate church-based outreach. This is the most powerful channel for reaching concentrated groups of Christian business owners who trust a common source.

### Urgency Mechanics

- **Public spot counter** on the website: show how many founding spots remain in real time (or update daily).
- **Email any pre-launch waitlist** when spots hit 100 remaining and again at 50.
- Do NOT create fake urgency. If the counter is accurate and the lifetime deal genuinely disappears at 500, the urgency is real and honest. Let it speak.

### Phase 2 Metrics to Watch

- Founding member signups per week
- Spots remaining
- Referral credits issued
- Conversion rate from Facebook Group to paid signup
- Which platform is driving the most conversions (use a UTM link or ask members at checkout "how did you find us?")

## Phase 3 — Ongoing Growth (Month 2+)

**Goal:** Transition from launch energy to sustainable, consistent growth. Shift from lifetime deal to annual memberships, activate the referral flywheel, and build a content engine that keeps driving inbound discovery.

### Annual Member Acquisition

Once the Founding 500 closes, the pitch changes — but it does not diminish. Annual membership at $59.99/year is still a fraction of what competitors charge. Key messages:
- "The founding spots are gone. But the mission isn't."
- "You can still be one of the first 1,000 members to build something together."
- Introduce tiered value: What has the directory done for current members? Gather and share results.

### Content Cadence

Build a repeatable weekly rhythm across platforms:

| Day | Platform | Content Type |
|-----|----------|--------------|
| Monday | Facebook | Devotional/faith + business thought (300–500 words) |
| Tuesday | Instagram | Member spotlight Reel or carousel |
| Wednesday | TikTok | Short educational or story video |
| Thursday | LinkedIn | Professional insight or data-backed post |
| Friday | Facebook + Instagram | Weekend call-to-action — "Find a Christian business this weekend" |

This is a one-person output rhythm James can realistically sustain or batch-produce in a single day per week.

### Referral Flywheel

The $4.99 credit per referred annual member is not just a discount mechanism — it is a word-of-mouth engine. Each member's renewal cost decreases as they refer. A member with five referrals essentially pays $35/year. Frame this as: "Help grow the community, and the community takes care of you."

- Remind members of their referral link in a monthly email.
- Celebrate milestones: "Our community just passed 700 members — thank you for helping build this."
- Create a shareable graphic for members to post: "I'm in the Cornerstone Directory — are you?"

### Organic SEO & Blog Content

The free tier allows visitors to browse and read the blog. This makes SEO-driven blog content a direct pipeline to paid membership.

Target blog topics:
- "How to find a Christian plumber / attorney / financial advisor near you"
- "Why I choose to support Christian-owned businesses"
- "What does the Bible say about commerce and business relationships?"
- "The Cornerstone Directory story — built on Psalm 118:22"

Each post should end with a soft CTA to list a business or browse the directory.

### Partnership & Affiliate Growth

- Approach Christian podcasts (business-focused): Faith Driven Entrepreneur, The Christian Entrepreneur, Theology of Business. Offer a unique promo code for their listeners.
- Reach out to Christian business coaches and consultants — offer them a small affiliate arrangement or simply ask them to share with their audience if they believe in the mission.
- Submit the directory to Christian gift guide lists, resource pages, and denominational business networks.

## Target Audiences

### Audience 1 — The Christian Small Business Owner

**Profile:** A Christian who owns or operates a small business (contractor, consultant, retailer, service professional) and sees their work as an expression of faith. They want their business to reflect their values, and they prefer to do business with people who share those values when possible.

**Pain point:** No easy way to find or be found by other believers in business. May be currently invisible to potential Christian clients.

**How to reach them:** Facebook (personal profile and group posts that get shared), church announcement boards, local Christian business networks, word of mouth.

**Message that resonates:** "Your business deserves to be found by people who share your values. And you deserve to find them."

---

### Audience 2 — The Faith-Driven Consumer

**Profile:** A Christian consumer — individual or family — who actively or occasionally seeks out Christian-owned businesses to support. They may tithe, give to missions, and naturally prefer businesses they know operate with integrity.

**Pain point:** No single trusted place to find Christian-owned businesses. Reliance on word-of-mouth or Google with no way to filter by faith.

**How to reach them:** Instagram and TikTok (they're browsing), church bulletin boards and Facebook groups, Christian parenting communities, homeschool networks.

**Message that resonates:** "Every dollar you spend is a vote for someone's values. Spend it with people who share yours."

---

### Audience 3 — The Christian Professional in a Network Context

**Profile:** A business-minded Christian — often mid-career, active in a church, maybe in a Christian men's or women's group, who already believes in the principle of "buying Christian" but hasn't had a tool that makes it easy. They may have attended a Christian business networking event but found no lasting infrastructure.

**Pain point:** The infrastructure for Christian business community is fragmented — networking events are one-offs, Facebook groups go quiet, nothing aggregates it all.

**How to reach them:** LinkedIn, Christian Chamber of Commerce, Faith Driven Entrepreneur community, church small groups.

**Message that resonates:** "You believe in this. Here's the infrastructure that finally makes it real."

## Key Messages

These five messages should appear consistently across all content, in various forms, in James's voice.

**1. Christians should be able to find each other in business.**
This is the most direct and universal message. It names the problem plainly. It speaks to both the business owner who wants to be found and the consumer who wants to find.

**2. The stone the builders rejected has become the cornerstone.**
Psalm 118:22 is not just a name — it is a theology. Christian business has been sidelined, undervalued, and scattered. Cornerstone Directory is the infrastructure that puts it at the center. Use this scripture not as a church quote but as a lived conviction.

**3. $59.99 lifetime. Competitors charge $399–$699 per year. That math matters.**
Be specific. Never vague. The price comparison is one of the clearest, most persuasive facts in this entire story. Use it plainly and let it land.

**4. This is a founding story. You either get to be part of the beginning, or you don't.**
The Founding 500 opportunity is real and finite. Scarcity is not manufactured here — it is honest. People who believe in the mission should know this window exists and that it closes.

**5. Your membership is not just a listing — it's a vote for a different kind of economy.**
Every member who joins is choosing to say that shared values matter in commerce. Frame membership as participation in something bigger than a business transaction.

## Church & Community Outreach

This channel is arguably the most powerful and most underused in Christian business marketing. Churches are trusted communities with concentrated populations of Christian business owners and consumers. The key is being genuine — not treating the church as a marketing channel, but as a community James is already part of.

### Tactic 1 — Personal Outreach to Pastors and Elders

James should personally reach out (not mass email) to 10–20 pastors and church leaders he has a relationship with or can be warmly introduced to. The ask is simple and low-stakes: "I'm building something for Christian business owners in our community. Would you be willing to share it with your congregation or look it over yourself?"

Offer a complimentary founding membership to the pastor and to the church (if it operates any business or marketplace ministry). Never frame this as a sponsorship or paid relationship — keep it relational and genuine.

### Tactic 2 — Church Bulletin and Announcement Board

Prepare a simple one-page bulletin insert (clean, not slick) that explains the directory, the Founding 500 opportunity, and how to join. Churches that allow member announcements will often include this if James is a member or has a relationship with a member.

### Tactic 3 — Christian Business Fellowships and Men's/Women's Groups

Many churches have marketplace or business fellowships that meet monthly. James should request to speak for 5–10 minutes at one of these gatherings — not a sales pitch, but a short story about why he built this and an invitation to join. A QR code on a simple card is all the collateral needed.

### Tactic 4 — Christian Business Facebook Groups

There are hundreds of Facebook groups for Christian entrepreneurs, Christian business women, faith-driven marketers, and local Christian business networks. James should join the most relevant ones, participate genuinely for a week or two, and then make a single, honest post introducing the directory. No spam. No repeated posts. One genuine introduction.

High-value groups to target:
- Faith Driven Entrepreneur (FDE) community spaces
- Christian Business Women communities
- Local city/region Christian business groups
- Homeschool family groups (these have high overlap with faith-based purchasing behavior)

### Tactic 5 — Denominational Networks

Some denominations have formal business or marketplace ministries. Southern Baptist, AG, non-denominational evangelical networks, and others sometimes have directories of their own. Reach out to these structures not to compete but to partner — offer them a free group listing or affiliate relationship.

### LinkedIn Post 1 — The Business Case for a Christian Business Directory

There are an estimated 40 million Christians in the American workforce.

A significant share of them own or operate small businesses. Many of them actively prefer to hire, partner with, and purchase from other Christians when they can.

And yet, in 2024, there is no well-designed, accessible, trusted infrastructure for them to find each other.

The largest Christian business directories charge $400–$700/year for a listing. That price point immediately excludes most of the small business owners who would benefit most: the sole proprietors, the trades, the independent service providers, the consultants and coaches who make up the bulk of the Christian business community.

This is a market gap, not a niche quirk.

The demand is real. Survey data consistently shows that a meaningful percentage of consumers factor shared values into purchasing decisions. "Certified Christian" may not have the same infrastructure as "Certified B Corp," but the underlying behavior — consumers choosing businesses that reflect their values — is well-documented.

I've spent the last year building Cornerstone Directory: a Christian business directory where business owners list their services and consumers find them. The model is simple. The mission is straightforward. The pricing is accessible.

We're currently in our founding member phase — 500 lifetime spots before the model moves to annual subscription. The goal was always to build something people could afford first, and grow from there.

The business case is real. The community exists. It just hasn't had the right infrastructure.

Happy to discuss this space with anyone building in the faith and commerce intersection.

### LinkedIn Post 2 — The Founding Member Opportunity

Founding member programs work for two reasons:

First, the economics: the person who joins early, when the platform is unproven, is taking on more risk than the person who joins later, when the value is established. The lower price acknowledges that risk honestly.

Second, the identity: founding members aren't just customers. They're stakeholders. Their participation shapes what the platform becomes. Early directories with strong founding cohorts grow differently than those assembled through ad spend alone.

Cornerstone Directory's Founding 500 is structured with both of these in mind.

The offer: $59.99 one-time, for life. No renewals. Ever.

After 500 spots, it moves to $59.99/year — still well below the $399–$699 annual rates competitors charge. But the lifetime option closes permanently.

For a Christian business owner, the ROI math is simple: if a single new client finds you through the directory in the first year, the membership has paid for itself many times over. This is not a speculative bet — it's a modest first step into a growing infrastructure for Christian commerce.

I'm the founder. I built this in southern Utah, out of a genuine belief that Christian business owners deserve better tools and a stronger community.

If you're a Christian business professional, or you advise them, this is worth a look.

### LinkedIn Post 3 — The Economic Argument for Christians Supporting Christians

There is a concept in behavioral economics called "values-based purchasing" — the tendency of consumers to factor the perceived values of a business into their buying decisions.

It shows up in ESG investing. It shows up in the growth of fair trade. It shows up in the B Corp movement, which has seen thousands of companies pursue certification specifically because consumer and B2B buyers increasingly want to know how a company operates, not just what it sells.

The Christian community has operated on this principle informally for decades — asking friends at church for contractor recommendations, preferring the Christian-owned insurance agency in town, supporting the business of a fellow small group member.

What has been missing is the infrastructure.

Word-of-mouth networks are powerful but they don't scale. They're also invisible to the believer who just moved to a new city, or the entrepreneur who doesn't yet know anyone in local business circles.

Cornerstone Directory is an attempt to give that informal network a formal address.

The argument is not sentimental — it's structural. A concentrated community of buyers who trust each other represents a genuine economic advantage for businesses within that community. Lower customer acquisition cost. Higher lifetime value. Referrals driven by shared identity, not just product satisfaction.

This is not about excluding anyone. It is about building something specific enough to be useful, and large enough to matter.

We are in the founding phase. The business case is straightforward. The community is ready.
